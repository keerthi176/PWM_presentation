Section 1

PWM compresses analog intent into timed digital pulses.

Continuous analog control can be approximated by rapidly alternating between full ON and full OFF states. The load responds primarily to the average energy delivered over each switching period.

Key equation

Vavg ≈ Duty Cycle × Vsupply for resistive or filtered loads.

Why engineers use it

Switching devices operate efficiently near the ON and OFF extremes, so power loss is often lower than in linear control.

What changes system behavior

Load type, switching frequency, dead time, rise/fall time, filtering, and control-loop bandwidth.

Duty Cycle D = Ton / T

Fraction of each period spent in the ON state.

Period f = 1 / T

Higher frequency can reduce ripple, but increases switching stress.

Section 2

Mathematical model: average value comes from time weighting.

For a periodic signal that remains at Vs during Ton and at 0 V during Toff, the average output over one period follows directly from time-domain integration.

01

T = Ton + Toff

02

Vavg = (1 / T) ∫0T v(t) dt

03

Vavg = (1 / T) [(Vs)(Ton) + (0)(Toff)]

04

Vavg = Vs × (Ton / T) = Vs × D

Example 12 V × 0.25 = 3 V

At 25% duty cycle, the average delivered voltage is 3 V for a filtered or averaging load.

Engineering note Average is not instant

The waveform remains discontinuous. Smooth system behavior appears only when the load averages or filters the switching signal.

Section 3

Proposition

For an ideal PWM waveform applied to a linear low-pass or energy-averaging load, the low-frequency output component is proportional to duty cycle.

Proof sketch

  1. Represent the input over one switching period as a rectangular periodic waveform.
  2. The DC term of that waveform equals its average over one period.
  3. That average is Vs × D, from the time-domain integral.
  4. A low-pass filter or an inductive-mechanical load suppresses high-frequency switching harmonics.
  5. The dominant observed output therefore tracks the DC term, which is proportional to duty cycle.

The result provides engineering rigor suitable for presentations in embedded systems, control theory, and power electronics.

Section 4

Waveform lab with horizontal sub-slides.

Horizontal sub-slides compare representative duty-cycle conditions and their resulting engineering implications.

Panel A

Low duty cycle: low average energy

A narrow ON interval delivers short bursts of energy separated by longer OFF intervals. This condition is associated with low LED brightness or light motor torque demand.

  • Average output from 12 V source is about 1.2 V.
  • Ripple becomes more visible if frequency is too low.
  • Perceptible flicker is likely in visual systems at poor frequency choices.
Panel B

Balanced waveform: classic demonstration case

At 50%, the ON and OFF intervals are equal. This case is commonly used to illustrate timer behavior, waveform symmetry, and midpoint average power delivery.

  • Average output is about 6 V from a 12 V source.
  • Symmetric timing simplifies explanation, not always system optimization.
  • Current ripple still depends on load inductance and filter design.
Panel C

High duty cycle: near-full delivery

The waveform remains ON for most of the switching period, with only short OFF intervals. Loads with inertia or filtering may therefore respond as if driven by a nearly steady high level.

  • Average output is about 10.8 V from a 12 V source.
  • Thermal and switching limits still matter even if average output is high.
  • Control resolution near rail conditions becomes important.

Section 6

LED behavior remains binary even when brightness appears continuous.

Based on the referenced PWM explainer video, the LED should be understood as a device that is either fully ON or fully OFF at each instant. Intermediate brightness appears only because the ON time changes within each period.

State A

0% duty cycle

The LED receives no ON interval during the period, so it remains OFF for the entire cycle.

State B

50% duty cycle

The LED alternates between full ON and full OFF. The eye integrates the rapid switching and perceives a medium brightness level.

State C

100% duty cycle

The LED remains ON for the complete period, producing maximum average optical output.

Key observation

The video-linked explanation emphasizes outcomes of different duty cycles rather than intermediate analog voltage levels at the LED itself. The waveform changes in width, while the LED switching state stays binary.

Section 7

Where PWM becomes system-critical.

01

Motor control

Duty cycle regulates average armature voltage, enabling speed and torque control in DC drives, BLDC stages, and H-bridge systems.

02

Switch-mode power supplies

PWM governs energy transfer in buck, boost, and inverter topologies, directly affecting efficiency, ripple, and electromagnetic behavior.

03

LED dimming

Average optical output changes with duty cycle while instantaneous drive remains digital, preserving efficient switching behavior.

04

Audio and actuation interfaces

PWM is used in class-D amplification, buzzers, and compact actuation interfaces where switching signals are filtered or mechanically averaged.

Section 8

PWM generation and utilization in one visual chain.

PWM begins as a timing comparison problem inside a controller and becomes useful only after the switching waveform interacts with a power stage, a filter, and a physical load.

Reference Speed / brightness / voltage target
Timer + Counter Periodic ramp or counter base
Compare Logic High while counter < compare
Power Stage MOSFET / driver / bridge
Load Response Motor, LED, converter, audio load

Actual generation

The PWM output stays high until the timer count crosses the compare threshold, after which it turns low for the remainder of the period.

Actual utilization

The switching waveform is then converted into usable electrical, optical, mechanical, or acoustic behavior by filtering, inertia, or energy storage.

Section 9

Hardware PWM generation inside an MCU.

In a microcontroller, PWM is typically generated by a timer peripheral whose counter is compared against programmed register values. The output pin changes state automatically when compare events occur.

MCU TIMER BLOCK
Timer Core PSC -> CNT -> ARR Clock division and counter progression
Comparator Compare CNT with CCR1 Generates match event inside hardware
Output Unit PWM mode + channel driver Updates pin state without CPU bit-banging
Live Simulation Counter, compare, and output state
CNT 000
CCR1 250
OUT LOW
PSC Prescaler register

Divides the base clock before the timer counts.

ARR / TOP Period register

Defines the PWM period or wrap value.

CCR1 Compare register

Defines the duty threshold within the period.

CCER / COM bits Channel enable + mode

Enables the pin output and selects PWM polarity/mode.

1. Clock division

timer_clk = fclk / (PSC + 1)

2. Period setup

fpwm = timer_clk / (ARR + 1)

3. Duty setup

duty = CCRx / (ARR + 1)

4. Hardware action

The channel output stays active while CNT < CCRx, then toggles automatically.

Register-level implementation pattern

TIMx_PSC  = 79;
TIMx_ARR  = 999;
TIMx_CCR1 = 250;

TIMx_CCMR1 |= PWM_MODE_1;
TIMx_CCER  |= CH1_ENABLE;
TIMx_CR1   |= TIMER_ENABLE;

This sequence configures the timer clock division, period, compare threshold, PWM mode, channel enable, and final timer start. Once enabled, the waveform is generated without CPU toggling.

Section 10

PWM in embedded systems: both hardware and software matter.

A complete embedded-systems view includes both the microcontroller peripherals that generate PWM in hardware and the firmware layers that compute, schedule, and supervise duty-cycle updates.

Hardware use

Timer and peripheral side

  • MCU timers generate PWM without continuous CPU intervention.
  • Output compare units define duty cycle with deterministic timing.
  • Complementary PWM and dead time are used in motor drives and power stages.
  • DMA or event systems can feed waveform updates at high rate.
  • Pins route PWM into LEDs, fans, H-bridges, buck converters, and servo outputs.
Software use

Firmware and control side

  • Control loops compute new duty values from sensor feedback.
  • RTOS tasks or ISRs decide when updates are safe and synchronized.
  • Calibration tables linearize nonlinear loads such as LEDs and motors.
  • Fault software clamps PWM during overcurrent, overtemperature, or undervoltage events.
  • Communication stacks expose PWM setpoints for UI, diagnostics, and remote control.

Section 11

Choosing PWM settings is mostly a tradeoff problem.

Practical PWM design is governed by competing constraints rather than by definitions alone.

Raise frequency

Smaller filters, smoother output, reduced low-frequency artifacts.

Cost: more switching loss and potential EMI stress.

Increase resolution

Finer duty-cycle control, improved low-speed or low-brightness behavior.

Cost: timer limits and control-update complexity.

Add filtering

Cleaner analog-like output, less ripple delivered to the load.

Cost: slower response, larger components, more design tuning.

Protect the switch

Dead time, gate shaping, and layout discipline reduce shoot-through and ringing.

Cost: added implementation detail and timing calibration.

Section 12

PWM design checklist for a real system review.

Define the load model: resistive, inductive, capacitive, or mixed.

Select frequency based on ripple, acoustics, EMC, and switch capability.

Validate thermal behavior at worst-case duty cycle and ambient condition.

Quantify timer resolution and control-loop update limits.

Review layout, current return paths, and gate-drive integrity.

Measure waveform quality on hardware, not only in simulation.

Final Frame

PWM is simple in principle and demanding in implementation.

That tension is exactly why it remains one of the most useful control techniques in embedded and power electronics. The subject combines a simple switching concept with nontrivial design decisions in implementation, control, and system integration.
